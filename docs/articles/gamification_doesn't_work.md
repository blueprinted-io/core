# Why Your Gamification Doesn't Work (And Untappd's Does)
### What a beer app understands about motivation that your LMS doesn't

Before diagnosing why corporate gamification fails so consistently, it's worth asking a question that almost nobody asks first: what is gamification actually for?

The answer most organisations are working from, implicitly or explicitly, is that gamification is an incentive to complete an activity. Finish the module, get the badge. Hit the target, unlock the reward. Hit enough targets, fill enough progress bars, and the system declares you done.

That answer is wrong, and wrong in a way that makes everything built on top of it wrong too.

Gamification's actual function is to incentivise interaction with a system. Not completion of a task. Interaction with a system. It is a tool to drive curiosity, to pull people deeper into something, to reward the kind of engagement that produces genuine familiarity over time. The moment you repurpose it as a mechanism to push people through a checklist, you have already failed, regardless of how well the badges are designed or how enthusiastically the rollout is announced.

---

## It Isn't Even One Thing

There is a prior problem worth naming before anything else. Gamification, as the term is commonly understood, is not one thing. It is two distinct things that have been travelling under the same name long enough that most design conversations start from a position of confusion.

The first is the use of badges, rewards and recognition systems to answer identity questions and drive engagement with a system. The second is the design of learning experiences that trigger the same intrinsic reward responses one gets from playing games: challenge calibrated to skill, meaningful consequence, the satisfaction of mastery.

These are separate disciplines with separate design principles, separate failure modes, and separate measures of success. Conflating them has produced a field where a compliance badge and a well-designed simulation exercise are treated as variations of the same thing. They are not. This article is primarily about the first. The second deserves its own conversation.

---

## The Human Need Underneath It

We don't name it often, but one of the stranger side effects of living in data-saturated organisations is that humans have developed an increasingly loud need to ask: who am I?

It sounds like a philosophical question. In practice it's a design opportunity that the most successful consumer products have understood for years. Buzzfeed built a media empire on it. Myers Briggs has survived decades of being thoroughly debunked because no amount of scientific criticism touches the reason people actually use it. Spotify Wrapped became a cultural moment and spawned a generation of imitators not because people wanted to know how many minutes of music they consumed, but because it handed them a story about themselves they could share.

The mechanism underneath all of these is the same. An external system reflecting something back at you that feels true. That is the precise psychological drive that gamification, at its best, is reaching for. The badge isn't meant to be a receipt. It's meant to be a mirror.

Corporate gamification fails the identity test completely. "Module Completion Certificate, Q3 Compliance Training 2024" tells you nothing about who you are. It tells you that you were present long enough for a system to register you. Nobody shares that. Nobody feels seen by it. The drive it was supposed to harness walks straight past it.

This matters because it forces a prior question. Before designing a single reward, before commissioning a single trophy graphic, you need to answer honestly: are you trying to get people to interact with a system, or are you trying to evidence that people completed an activity? If the answer is the latter, gamification is not the right tool. What you need is a compliance mechanism. Those are different problems with different solutions, and conflating them is where most corporate implementations go wrong before the first design conversation has finished.

There is a personal tension I want to articulate here. My further education ended with a degree in Commercial Music from the University of Paisley in 2006. I concluded long ago that I'd abandoned that line of enquiry and moved into the learning space. Two largely unrelated chapters.

Writing the Spotify Wrapped section I'm not so sure. Theodore Adorno's critique of the culture industry, that mass cultural products manufacture the illusion of individuality while delivering standardisation, is essentially a description of Spotify Wrapped, decades before the internet existed, let alone Spotify. Simon Frith sits on the other side of that argument: that music is fundamentally about identity formation, that we use it to answer who we are and where we belong.

The tension between those two positions is exactly what's at play in the gamification question. I apparently studied it twenty years before I needed it.

## What Failure Looks Like in Practice

Most people who have worked in organisations in the last decade have encountered the same artefact: a learning platform with a badge for completing a module, a points total that goes nowhere, and a leaderboard that nobody looks at after the first week.

The learner sees through it immediately. The badge represents nothing about who they are or what they can do. It represents the fact that they clicked through a series of screens without closing the browser. Rational responses are either to ignore the system entirely or to find the fastest path through it. Both are predictable. The system invited them.

The deeper problem is not lazy design. It is that the reward has been decoupled from any behaviour worth rewarding. The badge was bolted onto a process that was never designed around genuine engagement. It measures completion because completion is easy to measure, not because completion is the thing that matters.

## Three Systems That Got It Right, and Why

The clearest way to understand what gamification should be doing is to look at examples where it actually works, and ask precisely why.

**Untappd** is the commercial example that the learning and development world has almost entirely ignored, which is a significant oversight. Untappd is a social platform for logging and rating beer. Its badge system is widely regarded as one of the most effective implementations of gamification in any consumer product.

The elegance is not accidental, and it is not purely principled. Untappd sells behavioural data to breweries. Curious, engaged users who explore widely, log consistently, and interact genuinely are commercially valuable in a way that passive users are not. The incentive system had to drive deep interaction with the platform, not just sign-up completion or session frequency, because shallow engagement produces shallow data. Good incentive design and genuine user value aligned because the business needed curiosity, not compliance.

The result is a system where the reward and the behaviour are the same activity. You are not doing a separate thing to earn a badge. You are drinking interesting beer, paying attention, logging honestly, and the system notices what that says about you. The badges have personality and specificity. Not "you drank ten beers" but "you drank ten sour beers in winter." That is an identity signal. It tells you something about the kind of person you are.

The hidden badge is the key move. It appears only if you were paying attention in a way you were not optimising toward. You cannot game it without doing the thing it rewards. That is a design principle, not an accident. The system respects your intelligence enough to assume that genuine engagement is its own reward, and it surfaces recognition only when recognition is earned.

**PlayStation and Xbox trophy systems** sit in an instructive middle position. The trophy layer is not native to any individual game. It sits above the activity, applied across an entire platform. Done well, it works on the identity signal principle. Your trophy card says something about who you are as a player. Platinum trophies in particular generate a specific kind of pursuit: the people hunting them are not doing it for the badge. They are doing it for the statement it represents. "I completed this fully and I can prove it" is a claim about character, not a score.

Done badly, the system collapses into noise. One trophy for pressing start. One for finishing a tutorial. Developers who approach trophies as coverage rather than design produce exactly the same theatre as corporate compliance badges. Players notice immediately. The trophies are ignored or treated as a minor irritant. The difference between the two is whether the designer was thinking about identity and curiosity or just ticking boxes.

**Fallout's perk system** is the sharpest contrast to corporate badge design because perks do not just acknowledge what you have done. They change how you play. They are character traits expressed as mechanical advantages. "Educated" tells you something about who your character is in that world. Perks drive curiosity about what else the system contains, what combinations are possible, what kind of playthrough becomes available. They are rewards that open things rather than close them.

Compare that to "Module Completion Certificate, Q3 Compliance Training 2024." One makes you want to explore. The other makes you want to close the tab. The difference is not production value or graphic design. It is whether the reward is a signal about who you are or a receipt for time spent.

## The Harder Argument

Corporate gamification fails not because the people designing it are lazy or incompetent. It fails because it is being applied to the wrong problem in systems that do not respect the learner's intelligence.

Untappd works because it assumes you are a person with genuine taste doing something real, and it wants to know more about you. The compliance badge assumes you are a unit of regulatory risk to be processed. Learners know the difference immediately and respond accordingly. You cannot bolt a curiosity-driving system onto a process designed around minimum viable completion and expect curiosity to appear.

The Untappd business model is also worth sitting with honestly. The elegant badge design exists because deep engagement is commercially valuable. Before designing any incentive system, it is worth asking the same question Untappd had to answer: what does genuine engagement with this system actually produce, and why does it matter? If the honest answer is "evidence that a box was ticked," gamification will not help. If the honest answer is "people who understand this system deeply and interact with it consistently," then you have something worth designing around.

## What This Means in Practice

For anyone designing incentive systems in learning or knowledge work, the prior question has to come before any design decision: are you trying to drive curiosity and interaction, or evidence completion? If it is the latter, name it accurately and design the right tool for it. If it is the former, the design challenge is harder and more interesting.

You cannot directly reward quality of engagement because you cannot easily measure it. What you can do is watch for patterns of genuine interaction and name them when they appear. This is the hidden achievement as a design principle. Not a progress bar reaching a threshold. Not a counter hitting a number. The system noticing something true about how a person is working and surfacing it as recognition.

"Polymath." Awarded to someone who has reviewed content across three different domain areas. Not announced in advance. Not on a checklist. It appears because the system noticed something true about how that person works.

"Untouchable." Awarded to an author with ten consecutive pieces of content approved first time. Not a progress bar they were watching fill up. A recognition that arrives and tells them something about the quality of their work they might not have articulated themselves.

These names are not decoration. The name is the mechanism. "You have reviewed content across three domain areas" is a statistic. "Polymath" is a statement about who you are. The number is the trigger. The name is the reward. Strip the name back to the metric and you are back where you started.

The distinction sounds simple. In practice it requires a significant shift in how the problem is framed from the start. Most organisations commission gamification to drive completion rates. The question they should be asking is what behaviour, sustained over time, makes this system genuinely valuable, and how do we recognise the people who are producing it.

## The Principle

The only gamification that works is the kind where the reward is indistinguishable from genuine engagement with the thing itself. The system does not manufacture motivation. It notices something real and names it.

Before any of that is possible, you have to be honest about why you are doing it at all. Are you trying to get people to interact with a system, or complete an activity? That question, answered honestly, will tell you more about your design than anything else.

Most organisations, if they answer honestly, will realise they do not need gamification. They need compliance.

That is fine. It is not the same thing, and pretending otherwise is how you end up with a leaderboard nobody looks at and a badge for pressing start.

There is one final thing worth naming honestly. Gamification done correctly makes people want to spend more time interacting with a system. That is the entire point. A well designed incentive system drives curiosity, rewards depth, and pulls people back in.

Most business leaders want their staff spending as little time as possible in training.

Those two things cannot both be true. Looking at most corporate implementations with that tension in mind, the design starts to make a different kind of sense. The completion badge, the progress bar, the minimum viable reward for minimum viable engagement. It is not bad gamification. It is a precise reflection of what the organisation actually wants.

The more useful question is not why your gamification doesn't work. It is whether it is working exactly as intended.
